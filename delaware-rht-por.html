Delaware DHSS — Rural Health Transformation Program Overview

Delaware received $157.0M under the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program. The Delaware Department of Health and Social Services (DHSS) is administering the program with a focus on Kent and Sussex counties — the state's two primary rural regions, where approximately 40% of Delawareans live. Governor Meyer has set the program's north star: "A person's zip code should never dictate the quality of care they receive."

Delaware's program is built around four core initiatives. The most urgent — and the one with an active RFP right now — is the Rural Community Health Hubs model: community-based care infrastructure that extends health engagement between clinical visits for rural Kent and Sussex residents. The hub model is a services and operations contract, not a technology procurement. WHOOP's entry is as a named technology layer in the proposal of a prime bidder — specifically a hub operator who positions WHOOP as the continuous wearable health insights layer for community health workers and the populations they serve.

The other three program pillars — the Delaware Medical School (first 4-year medical school in the state, emphasizing primary care for rural communities), School-Based Health Centers, and additional RFPs expected spring 2026 — represent longer-horizon opportunities. For those, the appropriate play is to establish a relationship with DHSS contact ricky.williamson@delaware.gov now, before those solicitations are written.

WHOOP Positioning for Delaware — Hub Model Fit
WHOOP provides intelligent health guidance across sleep, recovery, strain, fitness, and longevity. For Delaware's hub model, the positioning is a dual-angle fit: (1) Workforce wellbeing — hub staff and community health workers are the frontline of community-based care delivery. WHOOP helps CHWs monitor their own resilience and recovery, reducing burnout in a demanding community health role. (2) Population health engagement — WHOOP extends health visibility for the rural Kent/Sussex populations served by the hub, providing continuous health insights between care touchpoints without requiring clinical infrastructure. Both angles require no clinical infrastructure and fit the low-resource deployment model of a rural community health hub.
