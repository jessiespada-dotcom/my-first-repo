Why Texas, Why Now

Texas received the maximum award under the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program — $281.3M over five years — to fund chronic disease management, workforce development, technology innovation, and capital infrastructure across rural Texas. The program is administered by the Texas Health and Human Services Commission (HHSC) and is structured around six initiatives, three of which represent direct entry points for WHOOP.

The single most important fact for WHOOP right now: Initiative 2 — the remote monitoring and technology innovation initiative — has no contractor, no RFP, and no scope finalized. HHSC is using Budget Period 1 to plan the solicitation. This window — where WHOOP can shape what the RFP asks for — closes the moment the RFP drops. Below are three distinct strategies for how WHOOP can approach Texas, evaluated by speed, scale, and probability of success.

The Three Plans
Plan A — Volume Play
Initiative 1 · $116M
Direct Hospital District Outreach
Go hospital by hospital to the ~80 rural districts receiving $750K each for chronic disease programming
The Approach

Each of the ~80 eligible hospital districts must submit a scope of work for their Initiative 1 funding and demonstrate chronic disease outcomes (reduced diabetes ED visits, increased disease management enrollment) to unlock pay-for-performance payments in Years 4–5. WHOOP positions as the chronic disease monitoring tool that makes those outcomes measurable and provable.

Key Actions
  • Map all 80 eligible hospital districts in counties ≤68,750 — build a target list
  • Build a WHOOP enrollment kit — a one-pager hospitals can drop directly into their HHSC funding proposal
  • Outreach to medical directors and hospital administrators — they are the decision-makers on how $750K gets spent
  • Position WHOOP as the outcomes layer — the tool that proves the program worked for pay-for-performance
Why It Works

This is the most immediate and tangible revenue path. 80 known targets, each with a defined budget and a need that WHOOP directly solves. The sales motion is familiar — it mirrors existing WHOOP enterprise outreach.

Plan B — Platform Play · Recommended
Initiative 2 · RFP Not Yet Launched
HHSC State-Level Technology Partnership
Partner with a Texas ACO or CIN to be named in the Initiative 2 remote monitoring solicitation before it goes to market
The Approach

Initiative 2 is explicitly scoped around "remote patient monitoring," "patient-facing consumer applications," and "technology-enabled access to care." The RFP has not launched — HHSC is writing the scope now. WHOOP engages the program team directly and identifies a Texas-based ACO or clinically integrated network to partner with as the prime contractor, with WHOOP as the named remote monitoring solution in their proposal.

Key Actions
  • Contact Anthony Hawley (HHSC Project Director) immediately to introduce WHOOP and request a meeting about Initiative 2 scope
  • Identify Texas ACOs and CINs actively preparing bids for the technology innovation initiative
  • Position WHOOP as the consumer wearable layer in a larger remote monitoring proposal — partner doesn't need to be exclusive
  • Ensure WHOOP is referenced in the RFP language — either by name or by category description that only WHOOP fits
Why It Works

One contract through HHSC means statewide distribution — access to all 202 rural Texas counties, not just 80 hospital districts. This is the highest-leverage play in the program. The window is narrow and closing as procurement planning progresses.

Plan C — Trojan Horse
Initiative 4 · $98.9M
Workforce Entry via Hospital Staff Wellness
Get WHOOP into rural hospitals through Initiative 4 workforce funds, then expand to the patient population
The Approach

Initiative 4 allocates $98.9M for recruiting and retaining rural healthcare workers — EMTs, community health workers, behavioral health providers, and primary care physicians. Hospitals must show sustainability plans for the workforce they build. WHOOP positions as a staff wellness and recovery monitoring benefit that reduces burnout and increases retention, packaged into a hospital's workforce incentive program.

Key Actions
  • Repackage WHOOP Unite for the rural healthcare workforce use case — frame it around provider burnout, shift recovery, and retention metrics
  • Target Initiative 4 subrecipients — hospitals actively building workforce pipelines are the fastest path to a signed deal
  • Use the existing WHOOP enterprise playbook — this sale mirrors existing corporate wellness deals, not a new motion
  • Land the staff deal → upsell to patients — once WHOOP is inside the hospital, expand to Initiative 1 chronic disease programming
Why It Works

This is the fastest path to a signed contract. It uses the existing WHOOP Unite enterprise motion, requires no new government relationships, and creates an immediate foot in the door at each hospital — with a clear expansion path to the patient population under Initiative 1.

My Recommendation
Run Plan B and Plan A in parallel — with Plan C as the immediate quick win. Plan B (state-level Initiative 2 partnership) is the highest-value play but requires the most lead time and a government BD motion that may be new for the enterprise team. Plan A (direct hospital outreach) runs simultaneously using a familiar enterprise sales motion — 80 known targets, clear budgets, immediate outreach. Plan C (workforce entry) should be activated first as it is the fastest path to a signed deal and requires no new sales motion — it gets WHOOP's foot in the door at rural hospitals while Plans A and B develop. The three plans are not mutually exclusive. They compound: a staff wellness deal (Plan C) opens the door to a patient chronic disease deal (Plan A), and both create social proof that supports the statewide Initiative 2 proposal (Plan B).

The Bigger Picture
Texas is one state. All 50 states received CMS Rural Health Transformation funding — $10B per year nationally, $50B over five years. Every state submitted its own proposal with its own initiative structure, its own hospital subrecipients, and its own technology procurement plan. Whatever playbook we build in Texas becomes the template for 49 more states. The goal is not just to win Texas — it is to build a repeatable, scalable GTM that can be deployed state by state as award notices are published. Texas is the proof of concept.
