The Opportunity

California was awarded $233,639,308.47 under the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program — the third-largest state award nationally — to fund chronic disease management, workforce development, and technology modernization across rural California. The program is administered by the California Department of Health Care Access and Information (HCAI) under the CalRHT brand and runs five budget periods through 2030. The RFA for subaward applicants is releasing Spring 2026 — the window is open now.

WHOOP's primary lane is Initiative 1: Transformative Care Model, which commits $112.2M to hub-and-spoke rural healthcare networks built around chronic disease management. The project narrative explicitly calls for "consumer-facing mobile-app-based technology with prevention-based programs" to target chronic disease conditions and "consumer remote patient self-monitoring devices" as allowable pool purchases for network grants. Approximately 16 hospitals will receive $35M in transformative payments, and an additional $39M in hub-and-spoke grants is flowing to rural networks — each of these organizations will need a technology vendor to execute their project scope.

The second major lane is Initiative 3: Technology & Tools, which allocates $35.7M to the Rural Technology Assistance Center (RTAC) and EHR adoption grants. The RTAC's stated mission includes: "Educate consumers and promote the use of tools and technologies that allow them to monitor their health, self-report to their providers" and "facilitate collaboration among rural health care providers to integrate patient self-reported and self-monitoring information into their workflows." This is the infrastructure play — if WHOOP is embedded in the RTAC as the recommended consumer wearable, it reaches every funded organization in the state simultaneously. No technology vendor has been selected for this role.

California's scale is the critical differentiator: 108 rural hospitals (including 39 Critical Access Hospitals), 279 Rural Health Clinics, 296 FQHCs, and 2.7 million rural residents across 57 of 58 counties. A single state-level partnership with HCAI — positioning WHOOP as the CalRHT-recommended consumer self-monitoring device — creates deployment at a scale that no individual hospital deal can approach. This is WHOOP's highest-leverage state in the entire national program.

Finally, Initiative 2: Workforce Development commits $62.4M to recruit and retain healthcare providers in rural California — including $54.17M in retention and relocation stipends and $7M in career pathway programs. Providers taking rural service commitments need a wellness infrastructure. WHOOP Unite positions naturally as the provider wellness benefit that supports recruitment by giving rural health systems a tangible, premium retention tool they can point to during hiring.

The Rural Technology Assistance Center ($23.55M) is being built specifically to identify, vet, and deploy consumer health technology tools across every CalRHT-funded organization in the state. If WHOOP is embedded in the RTAC as the recommended consumer self-monitoring device, it has state-level endorsement and flows to every funded organization simultaneously — without requiring WHOOP to run 683 individual sales cycles. National Context — California as the Platform State
California is the 3rd-largest award nationally ($233.6M) and the largest rural population of any state in the program (2.7M residents). The CalRHT strategy complements Texas (direct hospital district outreach) and Alaska (Targeted Innovation statewide pathway) and should become the model for the 10 largest state awards in the national $50B program. Key differences that make California distinct: the RTAC creates a state-level technology endorsement mechanism that no other state has built, the hub-and-spoke network structure creates clear network administrator targets, and the explicit consumer tech language in the project narrative means WHOOP is already described in the program design — before a single RFP has been written.
