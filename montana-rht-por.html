The Opportunity

Montana received $233,509,358.76 under the CMS Rural Health Transformation Program — the 4th-largest state award nationally — to fund rural healthcare transformation across 51 eligible Montana counties. The program runs five budget periods through 2030, with up to $1.2B total, and is administered by the Montana Department of Public Health and Human Services (DPHHS).

Montana's program structure is fundamentally different from every other state. There are NO subawards to individual hospitals or organizations. DPHHS is running ~8 competitive state-level RFPs and ~5 grant programs, all through Montana's eMACS procurement portal (bids.mt.gov), all by September 2026. This changes WHOOP's entire GTM approach.

Two RFPs are live RIGHT NOW. The CoE Implementation RFP (DPHHS-RFP-2026-0670R, $105.5M, deadline May 15) is the most critical: the winning contractor manages all subcontractors, deploys population health interventions, handles technology upgrades across the program, and disburses funds to rural facilities. WHOOP's immediate play is to get named as a technology subcontractor in a CoE Implementation bidder's proposal — before May 15. The CoE Analytics RFP (DPHHS-RFP-2026-0671, $11.6M) closes even sooner: May 1.

WHOOP's primary direct lane is Initiative 5: Deploy modern health care technologies to guide rural health interventions. This initiative has NOT yet released an RFP — it is a future wave procurement. When it drops, WHOOP submits directly as a primary technology vendor. The Initiative 5 project narrative language (specific technology types named) is not yet publicly available — this is a gap to close before the RFP releases. Monitor ruralhealth.mt.gov.

The workforce angle remains: Initiative 1 workforce retention and upskilling explicitly includes "wellness and resilience programs for HCPs in rural communities" as a funded activity — WHOOP Unite via the existing enterprise motion.

Montana's Structural Difference — Why the Playbook Changes
No Subawards
Montana does NOT distribute funds to individual hospitals the way Texas (~80 hospital districts) or Alaska (~1,800 LOI orgs) does. DPHHS runs state-level competitive RFPs. There is no list of funded organizations to cold-call. The entire market is accessed through state contracts and the vendors who win those contracts.
CoE Implementation
The $105.5M CoE Implementation contractor is Montana's central operator — managing subcontractors, deploying technology, disbursing funds. Getting written into that contractor's proposal is the equivalent of winning a state-level partnership. It reaches every rural facility in Montana through one relationship.
Initiative 5 — Direct Lane
Initiative 5 is WHOOP's primary direct procurement lane. When the RFP releases, WHOOP responds as a primary technology vendor. No intermediary. No subcontractor positioning. This is the clean, direct path — but it requires knowing what specific technology types Initiative 5 names when the RFP drops.

Montana's Unique Structure vs. Other States
Unlike Texas (direct outreach to ~80 hospital districts), Alaska (LOI-funded organizations), or California (RTAC state endorsement mechanism), Montana has no mechanism for direct hospital outreach under RHTP. Every dollar flows through state-level contracts. This is not a weakness — it's a concentration of leverage. One subcontractor position in the CoE Implementation contract reaches every rural facility in Montana. And when Initiative 5 releases its technology RFP, WHOOP competes directly for a state-level technology contract — potentially the single largest direct technology procurement in any state's RHTP program.
